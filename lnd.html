Overview

Mapping the invisible forces behind Kerala's most dangerous terrain.

Landslides during monsoons destroy homes, farms, and roads in Idukki due to steep hills, weak clay soils, heavy rainfall, and human activities like road cutting. Existing hazard maps are too general — this project tests how strongly natural and human factors spatially correlate with real landslide locations, using QGIS and open-source datasets to produce actionable, localised risk intelligence. Inspired by witnessing the 2018 Kerala floods firsthand.

Idukki is one of Kerala's most landslide-prone districts — steep Western Ghats terrain, clay-rich soils, and some of India's highest monsoon rainfall create a system primed for failure. Yet official risk maps remain coarse, failing to capture the hyperlocal interactions between slope, soil saturation, and land-use change that actually trigger events.

This project asks a precise question: which factor combinations most reliably predict where past landslides occurred? The answer has direct implications for early warning placement, infrastructure planning, and reforestation priorities.

Understanding the spatial logic of disaster — not just where, but why there.

Land Cover

Tree-covered areas dominate Idukki, with grassland and shrubland in scattered patches. Landslide points cluster centrally, most at transitions between forest and grassland — exactly where root systems weaken and runoff intensifies.

Soil Type

Clay dominates southern and eastern regions. Sand sits in northern and western areas. Landslides predominantly overlap clay-rich zones — high plasticity means poor drainage and slope instability when saturated.

Rainfall & Slope

Rainfall ranges from ~3,488 mm in western/central areas to ~482 mm at eastern edges. Slope risk peaks in hilly terrain. Landslides appear on moderate-to-high slopes with mixed rainfall intensities — the combination, not either factor alone, drives failure.

Risk Assessment

Multi-factor
overlay

What the overlay reveals

Overlaying high-risk slope (red), clay soil (brown), and heavy rainfall (dark blue) zones reveals clear clustering of past landslide events in areas where all three factors simultaneously overlap — confirming that no single factor is sufficient.

Human amplification

Road-cutting and deforestation patterns trace visibly onto the risk surface — human activity in the Western Ghats consistently amplifies natural vulnerabilities, concentrating failure along infrastructure corridors.

05
Validation

Against official
records

KSDMA Comparison

The model's high-risk zones align closely with KSDMA's official susceptibility map. Integrating soil texture and rainfall layers refines these boundaries — producing more localised predictions where the official map generalises across district-scale zones.

Accuracy signal

The majority of verified historical landslide points fall within the top-risk tier of the multi-factor overlay — confirming that the combined factor approach outperforms any single-variable model for Idukki's terrain.

06
Key Findings

What the
data said

F.01
Triple-factor convergence

Steep slopes (>30°) combined with clay-dominant soil and heavy monsoon rainfall form Idukki's highest-risk signature — zones where all three overlap account for the majority of verified landslide events.

F.02
Forest-edge vulnerability

Landslides cluster disproportionately at forest-to-grassland transition zones — where canopy cover and root systems weaken, surface runoff accelerates, and slope cohesion decreases during sustained rainfall.

F.03
Human factors amplify risk

Deforestation and road-cutting corridors visibly concentrate failure risk along infrastructure routes — human activity on naturally vulnerable terrain consistently produces the highest-density landslide clusters.

F.04
Actionable impact

Results directly enable planners to prioritise clay-rich steep zones for early-warning sensor placement, soil stabilisation works, and targeted reforestation — turning spatial analysis into prevention strategy.
