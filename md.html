Overview

Where classical Indian gesture meets real-time machine learning.

Mudra Detection explores the intersection of traditional art and modern computation by translating classical Bharatanatyam gestures into interactive digital experiences. Using ml5.js and p5.js, the project recognises five mudras in real time and plays corresponding Carnatic varnams — turning cultural expression into an engaging, sensory learning tool.

Classical Indian dance forms like Bharatanatyam carry rich gestural vocabularies — mudras — each with specific cultural and spiritual meaning. Yet this knowledge remains largely inaccessible outside formal training. The challenge was to make this vocabulary discoverable through technology without reducing or misrepresenting it.

Through this project, I discovered how machine learning can preserve and reinterpret culture, while also confronting the ethical responsibilities of dataset creation, representation, and model accuracy. It deepened my understanding of computational workflows and how technology can support creative education when approached thoughtfully.

Machine learning as a means of cultural preservation — not replacement.

Workflow & Process

From image
to gesture

Dataset Collection

Images of five mudras performed by multiple people, organised into folders and uploaded to Google Drive. Python generated path2.csv with image names and paths.

Keypoint Extraction

The ml5.js handpose model extracted 21 hand keypoints per image, producing hand_keypoints.csv. An extra 43rd point ("which hand") was identified and removed to prevent NaN errors.

Model Training

A Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) was trained on the keypoints dataset, outputting model.json, model_meta.json, and model.weights.bin — the three files needed for live prediction.

Audio Integration

Carnatic varnam audio clips sourced from YouTube were integrated into p5.js, mapping each detected mudra to its corresponding piece in real time via webcam.

06
Challenges

Bugs, limits &
lessons

C.01
Extra Keypoint

Handpose detected 43 points instead of 42. The extra "which hand" point caused NaN errors in training graphs — fixed by ignoring that column in the CSV.

C.02
Local File Access

Browsers block direct local file access. Served model.json and audio via python -m http.server 8000 for proper testing.

C.03
CNN vs MLP

CNN training failed due to limited data. Switched to a Multi-Layer Perceptron, which performed reliably on the 21-keypoint input structure.

C.04
Dataset Gaps

Missing keypoint data during extraction. Resolved by generating path2.csv for accurate, validated keypoint extraction before training.

C.05
Detection Glitches

Mayura and Arala showed inconsistent results due to posture variation in training images. Hands with paint, charcoal, or dark backgrounds also failed detection.
